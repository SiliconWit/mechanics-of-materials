## üìö Fundamental Theory: Combined Stress Analysis

### Stress State at a Point

When multiple loads act simultaneously, the stress state becomes more complex:

<Card title="üìä Combined Normal Stress Formula" icon="document">
**Individual components:**
- From bending: $\sigma_{bending} = \frac{My}{I}$
- From axial load: $\sigma_{axial} = \frac{P}{A}$

**Combined normal stress:**
$$\sigma_x = \frac{P}{A} \pm \frac{My}{I}$$

**Physical Meaning:** Normal stresses from different loading types are algebraically combined, with tension positive and compression negative.
</Card>

<Card title="üåÄ Combined Shear Stress Formula" icon="document">
**Individual components:**
- From torsion: $\tau_{torsion} = \frac{Tr}{J}$
- From transverse shear: $\tau_{shear} = \frac{VQ}{It}$

**Combined shear stress:**
$$\tau_{total} = \tau_{torsion} + \tau_{shear}$$

**Physical Meaning:** Shear stresses from different sources are vectorially combined at each point in the cross-section.
</Card>

### Principal Stress Theory

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Principal Stresses">
    
    <Card title="üî± Principal Stress Formula" icon="document">
    $$\sigma_{1,2} = \frac{\sigma_x + \sigma_y}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x - \sigma_y}{2}\right)^2 + \tau_{xy}^2}$$
    

    **Where:**
    - $\sigma_1$ = Maximum principal stress (most tensile)
    - $\sigma_2$ = Minimum principal stress (most compressive)
    - $\sigma_x, \sigma_y$ = Normal stresses in x, y directions
    - $\tau_{xy}$ = Shear stress in xy plane
    
    **Physical Meaning:** Principal stresses represent the maximum and minimum normal stresses at a point, occurring on planes with zero shear stress.
    </Card>
    
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Maximum Shear Stress">
    
    <Card title="üåÄ Maximum Shear Stress" icon="document">
    **In-plane maximum shear:**
    $$\tau_{max} = \frac{\sigma_1 - \sigma_2}{2}$$
    
    **Absolute maximum shear:**
    $$\tau_{abs,max} = \frac{\sigma_{max} - \sigma_{min}}{2}$$
    
    **Physical Meaning:** Maximum shear stress occurs on planes oriented 45¬∞ from the principal stress directions. For 3D analysis, consider all three principal stresses.
    </Card>

### Equivalent Stress Calculations

<Card title="‚öôÔ∏è Von Mises Equivalent Stress" icon="document">
**General form:**
$$\sigma_{eq} = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 - \sigma_1\sigma_2 + \sigma_2^2}$$

**For combined bending and torsion:**
$$\sigma_{eq} = \sqrt{\sigma^2 + 3\tau^2}$$

**Physical Meaning:** Von Mises stress represents the equivalent uniaxial stress that produces the same distortion energy as the actual multi-axis stress state.
</Card>

<Card title="‚ö° Maximum Shear Stress Theory" icon="document">
$$\sigma_{eq} = 2\tau_{max} = \sigma_1 - \sigma_2$$

**Physical Meaning:** Tresca theory predicts failure when maximum shear stress reaches the shear strength of the material (typically yield strength/2).
</Card>

## üîß Application: Robotic Wrist Joint Analysis

Let's analyze a realistic wrist joint under combined loading.

:::note[Problem Statement]
**Industrial Robot Wrist Joint Analysis**

**What we need to determine:**
1. **Individual stress components** from bending, torsion, and axial loading
2. **Equivalent stress** using von Mises and maximum shear stress theories
3. **Safety factors** for both failure theories
4. **Design adequacy** for the specified loading conditions

**Key Question:** Can this wrist joint safely handle the combined bending moment of 150 N¬∑m and torque of 200 N¬∑m simultaneously without failure?
:::

<hr />

**System Parameters:**
- Industrial robot wrist joint (hollow circular shaft)
- Wrist shaft: D = 60 mm (outer), d = 40 mm (inner)
- Critical section length: 100 mm
- Material: Steel 4140 (**`œÉ_yield`** = 415 MPa, **`œÑ_yield`** = 240 MPa)
- **`Safety factor`**: 2.5
- Bending moment: **`M`** = 150 N¬∑m (from tool weight and cutting forces)
- Torsional moment: **`T`** = 200 N¬∑m (from joint rotation)
- Axial force: **`P`** = 1000 N (from joint preload)

<hr />

### Step 1: Calculate Section Properties

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal section property calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Cross-sectional area:**
   
   $$A = \frac{\pi(D^2 - d^2)}{4} = \frac{\pi(60^2 - 40^2)}{4} = 1571 \text{ mm}^2$$

2. **Second moment of area:**
   
   $$I = \frac{\pi(D^4 - d^4)}{64} = \frac{\pi(60^4 - 40^4)}{64} = 8.95 \times 10^5 \text{ mm}^4$$

3. **Polar moment of inertia:**
   
   $$J = \frac{\pi(D^4 - d^4)}{32} = \frac{\pi(60^4 - 40^4)}{32} = 1.79 \times 10^6 \text{ mm}^4$$

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 2: Calculate Individual Stress Components

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal individual stress calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Axial stress from preload:**
   
   $$\sigma_{axial} = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{1000}{1571} = 0.64 \text{ MPa}$$

2. **Bending stress at outer fiber:**
   
   $$\sigma_{bending} = \frac{Mc}{I} = \frac{150 \times 10^3 \times 30}{8.95 \times 10^5} = 50.3 \text{ MPa}$$

3. **Combined normal stress:**
   
   $$\sigma_x = \sigma_{axial} + \sigma_{bending} = 0.64 + 50.3 = 50.9 \text{ MPa}$$

4. **Torsional shear stress:**
   
   $$\tau = \frac{Tr}{J} = \frac{200 \times 10^3 \times 30}{1.79 \times 10^6} = 33.5 \text{ MPa}$$

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 3: Apply Failure Theories

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal failure theory calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Von Mises equivalent stress:**
   
   $$\sigma_{eq} = \sqrt{\sigma_x^2 + 3\tau^2} = \sqrt{50.9^2 + 3(33.5)^2} = \sqrt{2591 + 3368} = 77.2 \text{ MPa}$$

2. **Maximum shear stress theory:**
   
   For pure shear and normal stress state:
   $$\sigma_1 = \frac{\sigma_x}{2} + \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x}{2}\right)^2 + \tau^2} = 25.45 + \sqrt{647.5 + 1122.25} = 67.5 \text{ MPa}$$
   $$\sigma_2 = \frac{\sigma_x}{2} - \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x}{2}\right)^2 + \tau^2} = 25.45 - 42.05 = -16.6 \text{ MPa}$$

   $$\sigma_{eq,Tresca} = \sigma_1 - \sigma_2 = 67.5 - (-16.6) = 84.1 \text{ MPa}$$

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 4: Safety Factor Assessment

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal safety factor analysis**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Von Mises safety factor:**
   
   $$SF_{vonMises} = \frac{\sigma_{yield}}{\sigma_{eq}} = \frac{415}{77.2} = 5.38$$

2. **Tresca safety factor:**
   
   $$SF_{Tresca} = \frac{\sigma_{yield}}{\sigma_{eq,Tresca}} = \frac{415}{84.1} = 4.93$$

3. **Design assessment:**
   
   Both safety factors exceed the required SF = 2.5 ‚úÖ

</Steps>

</details>

## üéØ Advanced Analysis: Fatigue Under Combined Loading

### Fatigue Considerations

Real robotic systems experience cyclic loading:

**Alternating Stress Components:**
- Bending: Varies with tool orientation
- Torsion: Varies with joint positioning
- Combined: Creates complex stress cycles

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Equivalent Alternating Stress">
    
    **For von Mises equivalent stress:**
    $$\sigma_{eq,alt} = \sqrt{\sigma_{alt}^2 + 3\tau_{alt}^2}$$
    
    **For mean stress effects:**
    $$\sigma_{eq,mean} = \sqrt{\sigma_{mean}^2 + 3\tau_{mean}^2}$$
    
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Modified Goodman">
    
    **Fatigue failure criterion:**
    $$\frac{\sigma_{alt}}{S_e} + \frac{\sigma_{mean}}{S_{ut}} = \frac{1}{n}$$
    
    Where:
    - S_e = Endurance limit
    - S_ut = Ultimate tensile strength  
    - n = Safety factor for fatigue
    
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Design Guidelines">
    
    **Fatigue design rules:**
    1. Minimize stress concentrations
    2. Use appropriate surface finishes
    3. Consider size effects
    4. Apply fatigue safety factors (3-10)
    
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Optimization Strategies

**For over-designed joints (SF > required):**

1. **Reduce shaft diameter** ‚Üí Weight savings, faster dynamics
2. **Use hollow sections** ‚Üí Maintain strength, reduce inertia
3. **Optimize material** ‚Üí High strength-to-weight ratio
4. **Redesign load paths** ‚Üí Minimize combined loading

## üõ†Ô∏è Design Guidelines for Combined Loading

### Analysis Procedure

:::note[Combined Loading Design Process]
1. **Identify all loading components** (bending, torsion, axial)
2. **Calculate individual stress components** at critical points
3. **Apply appropriate failure theory** (von Mises recommended for ductile materials)
4. **Check both static and fatigue failure modes**
5. **Optimize geometry** if over-designed or under-designed
:::

### Theory Selection Guidelines

**Von Mises (Distortion Energy):**
- **Best for ductile materials** (steel, aluminum)
- **Accurate for complex stress states**
- **Widely accepted in engineering practice**

**Maximum Shear Stress (Tresca):**
- **Conservative predictions**
- **Simple calculations**
- **Good for preliminary design**

**Maximum Normal Stress:**
- **For brittle materials only**
- **Not suitable for combined loading**
- **Limited practical use**

### Common Applications

**Robotic Joints:** Combined bending and torsion dominant
**Drive Shafts:** Torsion with bending from misalignment
**Tool Holders:** Bending dominant with some torsion
**Gear Shafts:** Combined bending, torsion, and contact stresses
