By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

2. **Calculate** allowable loads based on yield strength criteria
3. **Compare** material properties (aluminum vs steel) for actuator applications

## 🔧 Real-World System Problem: CNC Z-Axis Actuator Shaft

<BionicText method="advanced" intensity="medium" class="highlight">
In a Computer Numerical Control (CNC) machine, the Z-axis actuator controls vertical tool movement. The actuator shaft must transmit precise forces while maintaining dimensional accuracy under varying loads.
</BionicText>

### System Description

**CNC Z-Axis Components:**
- **Lead Screw** (converts rotary motion to linear motion)
- **Actuator Shaft** (transmits axial forces to the tool)
- **Linear Bearings** (guide smooth motion)
- **Stepper/Servo Motor** (provides controlled rotation)

### The Critical Challenge

During machining operations:
- The actuator shaft experiences **axial tension** when the tool pulls upward
- Heavy workpieces create **axial compression** loads
- Precision requirements demand **minimal shaft deformation**

> **Engineering Question:** How do we select the right material and shaft diameter to ensure the CNC system maintains ±0.01 mm positioning accuracy under a maximum load of 5,000 N?

### Why Material Selection Matters

**Aluminum Shaft Benefits:**
- Lightweight (reduces system inertia)
- Good machinability
- Corrosion resistance
- Lower cost

**Steel Shaft Benefits:**
- Higher strength
- Greater stiffness (higher E)
- Better wear resistance
- Proven reliability

The choice affects system performance, cost, and precision.

:::note[CNC Actuator Design Trade-offs]
<Badge text="Precision" variant="success" />: Steel's higher stiffness reduces deformation  
<Badge text="Speed" variant="tip" />: Aluminum's lighter weight enables faster motion  
<Badge text="Cost" variant="note" />: Material and machining costs vary significantly  
<Badge text="Durability" variant="caution" />: Steel offers better long-term wear resistance
:::

## 📚 Fundamental Theory: Axial Stress and Strain

Building on Lesson 1's foundation, let's develop the theory needed to solve our actuator shaft problem.

### Axial Loading Conditions

When forces act **along** the centerline of a shaft:

<Card title="⚡ Axial Stress Formula" icon="document">
$$\sigma_{axial} = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{4F}{\pi d^2}$$

**Where:**
- $F$ = Axial force (N)
- $d$ = Shaft diameter (m)
- $A$ = Cross-sectional area (m²)

**Physical Meaning:** Stress is distributed uniformly across the cross-section when forces act along the centerline.
</Card>

### Axial Strain and Deformation

The shaft will elongate (tension) or compress (compression) by:

<Card title="📏 Axial Deformation Formula" icon="document">
$$\delta = \frac{FL}{AE} = \frac{\sigma L}{E}$$

**Where:**
- $\delta$ = Total deformation (m)
- $L$ = Shaft length (m)
- $E$ = Young's modulus (Pa)

**Physical Meaning:** Deformation is proportional to force and length, but inversely proportional to cross-sectional area and material stiffness.
</Card>

### Design Criteria for Actuator Systems

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Strength Criterion">
    
    **Prevent Material Failure:**
    
    <Card title="🔒 Strength Design Criterion" icon="document">
    $$\sigma_{applied} \leq \frac{\sigma_{yield}}{SF}$$
    
    **Where:**
    - SF = Safety Factor (typically 2-4 for CNC systems)
    
    **Purpose:** Ensures the applied stress remains well below the material's yield strength to prevent permanent deformation.
    </Card>
    
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Stiffness Criterion">
    
    **Limit Deformation:**
    
    <Card title="📐 Stiffness Design Criterion" icon="document">
    $$\delta = \frac{FL}{AE} \leq \delta_{allowable}$$
    
    **Where:**
    - $\delta_{allowable}$ = Maximum acceptable deformation
    - For precision systems: $\delta_{allowable}$ = 0.01-0.1 mm
    
    **Purpose:** Maintains dimensional accuracy by limiting elastic deformation under load.
    </Card>
    
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Material Properties">
    
    **Aluminum 6061-T6:**
    - $E$ = 70 GPa
    - $\sigma_{yield}$ = 270 MPa
    - Density = 2,700 kg/m³
    
    **Steel 1045:**
    - $E$ = 200 GPa  
    - $\sigma_{yield}$ = 530 MPa
    - Density = 7,850 kg/m³
    
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## 🔧 Application: CNC Actuator Shaft Analysis

Let's solve the actuator shaft problem step by step using our theoretical foundation.

:::note[Problem Statement]
**CNC Z-Axis Actuator Shaft Material Selection**

**What we need to determine:**
1. **Minimum shaft diameter** required for strength (both materials)
2. **Stiffness compliance** with deformation limits
3. **Material selection** based on strength vs. stiffness requirements
4. **Final design recommendations** considering weight and cost

**Key Question:** Should we use aluminum or steel for this actuator shaft, and what diameter is required to meet both strength and stiffness requirements?
:::

<hr />

**System Parameters:**
- CNC Z-axis positioning system
- Circular actuator shaft
- Maximum axial force: 5,000 N (tension)
- **`Safety factor`**: SF = 3
- Deformation limit: δ ≤ 0.05 mm
- Shaft length: L = 400 mm

**Material Options:**
- **Option A:** Aluminum 6061-T6 (E = 70 GPa, σ_yield = 270 MPa)
- **Option B:** Steel 1045 (E = 200 GPa, σ_yield = 530 MPa)

<hr />

### Step 1: Determine Aluminum and Steel Strengths

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal strength calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Determine Minimum Shaft Diameter (Strength) for Aluminum 6061-T6:**
   
   Allowable stress: $\sigma_{allowable} = \frac{\sigma_{yield}}{SF} = \frac{270}{3} = 90 \text{ MPa}$
   
   Required area: $A_{min} = \frac{F}{\sigma_{allowable}} = \frac{5,000}{90 \times 10^6} = 5.56 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^2$
   
   Minimum diameter: $d_{min} = \sqrt{\frac{4A_{min}}{\pi}} = 8.41 \text{ mm}$

2. **Determine Minimum Shaft Diameter (Strength) for Steel 1045:**
   
   Allowable stress: $\sigma_{allowable} = \frac{530}{3} = 177 \text{ MPa}$
   
   Required area: $A_{min} = \frac{5,000}{177 \times 10^6} = 2.82 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^2$
   
   Minimum diameter: $d_{min} = 6.00 \text{ mm}$

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 2: Check 10 mm diameter Shaft Stiffness Requirements

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal shaft requirements calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Aluminum shaft (d = 10 mm):**

   Area: $A = \frac{\pi \times (0.01)^2}{4} = 7.85 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^2$

   Deformation: $\delta = \frac{FL}{AE} = \frac{5,000 \times 0.4}{7.85 \times 10^{-5} \times 70 \times 10^9} = 0.364 \text{ mm}$

   ❌ **Fails stiffness requirement** (0.364 mm > 0.05 mm allowable)

2. **Steel shaft (d = 10 mm):**

   Deformation: $\delta = \frac{5,000 \times 0.4}{7.85 \times 10^{-5} \times 200 \times 10^9} = 0.127 \text{ mm}$

   ❌ **Still fails stiffness requirement** (0.127 mm > 0.05 mm allowable)

</Steps>

:::caution[Stiffness Dominance]
In precision applications, **stiffness requirements often exceed strength requirements by large margins**. This leads to over-designed components from a strength perspective.
:::

:::tip[Young's Modulus Impact]
Steel's <Badge text="2.86× higher E" variant="success" /> compared to aluminum significantly reduces deformation, but still insufficient for this precision requirement.
:::

</details>

### Step 3: Size for Stiffness Requirements

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal area and diameter calculations**</summary>

<Steps>
1. Area and diameter for aluminum

   Required area for aluminum: $A = \frac{FL}{\delta_{allowable} \times E} = \frac{5,000 \times 0.4}{0.05 \times 10^{-3} \times 70 \times 10^9} = 5.71 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2$

   Required diameter: $d = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times 5.71 \times 10^{-4}}{\pi}} = 27.0 \text{ mm}$
   
2. Area and diameter for steel

   Required area for steel: $A = \frac{5,000 \times 0.4}{0.05 \times 10^{-3} \times 200 \times 10^9} = 2.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2$

   Required diameter: $d = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times 2.0 \times 10^{-4}}{\pi}} = 16.0 \text{ mm}$
</Steps>

</details>

### Step 4: Final Design Comparison

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal design recommendations**</summary>

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="Aluminum 6061-T6 Shaft">
    **Required Diameter:** 27 mm  
    **Mass (L=400mm):** 0.62 kg  
    **Stress:** 8.7 MPa  
    **Deformation:** 0.05 mm  
    **Cost:** Lower material cost
  </Card>
  <Card title="Steel 1045 Shaft">
    **Required Diameter:** 16 mm  
    **Mass (L=400mm):** 0.63 kg  
    **Stress:** 24.9 MPa  
    **Deformation:** 0.05 mm  
    **Cost:** Higher material cost
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

:::tip[Engineering Design Decision]
**Recommended: Steel 1045 shaft (16 mm diameter)**

**Advantages:**
- <Badge text="69% smaller diameter" variant="success" /> (16 mm vs 27 mm)
- <Badge text="Compact design" variant="tip" /> fits space constraints
- <Badge text="Similar mass" variant="note" /> (0.63 kg vs 0.62 kg)
- <Badge text="Higher strength margin" variant="success" /> for future load increases

**Trade-off:** Slightly higher material cost for superior performance
:::

</details>

## 🎯 Material Selection Guidelines for Mechatronics

### When to Choose Aluminum

:::note[Aluminum Applications in Mechatronics]
✅ **Excellent for:**
- <Badge text="High-speed robotics" variant="tip" />: Lower inertia enables faster acceleration
- <Badge text="Thermal management" variant="note" />: 4× better thermal conductivity than steel
- <Badge text="Corrosive environments" variant="success" />: Natural oxide layer protection
- <Badge text="Weight-critical systems" variant="tip" />: Drones, portable equipment

❌ **Avoid when:**
- <Badge text="Ultra-precision required" variant="danger" />: Lower E causes excessive deflection
- <Badge text="High contact stresses" variant="caution" />: Lower hardness leads to wear
- <Badge text="Fatigue-critical" variant="caution" />: Lower fatigue strength than steel
:::

### When to Choose Steel

:::note[Steel Applications in Mechatronics]
✅ **Excellent for:**
- <Badge text="Precision systems" variant="success" />: High E minimizes deflection
- <Badge text="Load-bearing structures" variant="success" />: Superior strength and stiffness
- <Badge text="Durable mechanisms" variant="tip" />: Excellent fatigue and wear resistance
- <Badge text="Space-constrained designs" variant="note" />: Higher strength enables smaller sections

❌ **Avoid when:**
- <Badge text="Weight-sensitive applications" variant="caution" />: 3× heavier than aluminum
- <Badge text="High-frequency motion" variant="caution" />: Higher inertia limits acceleration
- <Badge text="Budget constraints" variant="note" />: Higher material and machining costs
:::

## 📋 Summary and Next Steps

In this unit, you learned to:

1. **Analyze** axial stress in actuator shafts using σ = F/A
2. **Calculate** deformation using δ = FL/(AE)
3. **Apply** both strength and stiffness criteria in design
4. **Compare** aluminum vs steel for precision applications

**Key Engineering Insights:**
- <Badge text="Stiffness ∝ E" variant="tip" />: Material stiffness is material-dependent
- <Badge text="Stiffness ∝ A" variant="note" />: Cross-sectional area linearly affects stiffness  
- <Badge text="Strength ∝ A" variant="success" />: Doubling area doubles load capacity
- <Badge text="Deformation ∝ 1/E" variant="caution" />: Higher E dramatically reduces deflection

:::danger[Critical Design Rule]
**Always check both strength AND stiffness criteria**. In precision mechatronics, stiffness requirements typically govern the design, leading to components that are over-designed for strength.
:::
