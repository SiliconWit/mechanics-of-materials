## üìö Fundamental Theory: Shear Force and Bending Moment

### Basic Beam Concepts

When a beam is loaded transversely, internal forces develop to maintain equilibrium:

**Shear Force (V):** Internal force that resists sliding <br/>
**Bending Moment (M):** Internal moment that resists rotation <br/>
**Normal Force (N):** Internal axial force (usually zero in pure bending)

### Sign Conventions

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Shear Force Convention">
    
    **Positive Shear Force (+V):**
    - Left side of cut pushed **up** ‚¨ÜÔ∏è
    - Right side of cut pushed **down** ‚¨áÔ∏è
    - Creates clockwise rotation tendency ‚Üª
    
    **Negative Shear Force (-V):**
    - Left side of cut pushed **down** ‚¨áÔ∏è
    - Right side of cut pushed **up** ‚¨ÜÔ∏è
    - Creates counterclockwise rotation tendency ‚Ü∫
    
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Bending Moment Convention">
    
    **Positive Bending Moment (+M):**
    - **Tension** on bottom fiber
    - **Compression** on top fiber
    - Beam curves upward (smile shape) üòä
    
    **Negative Bending Moment (-M):**
    - **Compression** on bottom fiber
    - **Tension** on top fiber  
    - Beam curves downward (frown shape) üòû
    
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Load Directions">

    - **Upward** point loads ‚¨ÜÔ∏è
    - **Upward** distributed loads ‚¨ÜÔ∏è
    - **Counterclockwise** applied moments ‚Ü∫
    
    **Negative Load:**
    - **Downward** point loads (most common) ‚¨áÔ∏è
    - **Downward** distributed loads (self-weight) ‚¨áÔ∏è

  </TabItem>

### Differential Relationships

The fundamental relationship between load, shear, and moment:

<Card title="üìè Beam Differential Equations" icon="document">
$$\frac{dV}{dx} = -w(x)$$

$$\frac{dM}{dx} = V(x)$$

**Where:**
- $w(x)$ = distributed load intensity (N/m)
- $V(x)$ = shear force at position x (N)  
- $M(x)$ = bending moment at position x (N¬∑m)

**Physical Meaning:** These equations show how load affects shear force rate of change, and how shear force affects bending moment rate of change.
</Card>

### Common Beam Equations

<Card title="üõ°Ô∏è Cantilever Beam - Shear Force" icon="document">
**For cantilever with distributed load w and point load P at tip:**

$$V(x) = -(wL + P) + w \cdot x$$

**Where:**
- $w$ = Distributed load intensity (N/m)
- $L$ = Beam length (m)
- $P$ = Point load at tip (N)
- $x$ = Distance from fixed support (m)

**Physical Meaning:** Shear force starts negative (total downward load) and becomes less negative as distributed load is "taken up" along the length.
</Card>

<Card title="‚öñÔ∏è Cantilever Beam - Bending Moment" icon="document">
**For cantilever with distributed load w and point load P at tip:**

$$M(x) = -M_{base} + R_y \cdot x - w \cdot x \cdot \frac{x}{2}$$

**Where:**
- $M_{base}$ = Reaction moment at fixed support
- $R_y$ = Vertical reaction force
- Second term = Moment due to reaction force
- Third term = Moment due to distributed load

**Physical Meaning:** Bending moment varies quadratically due to distributed loading. Negative values indicate sagging (compression on top, tension on bottom).
</Card>

:::tip[Key Insights]
1. **Slope of shear diagram = negative of applied load**
2. **Slope of moment diagram = shear force value**
3. **Maximum moment occurs where shear = 0**
4. **Point loads cause jumps in shear diagrams**
5. **Applied moments cause jumps in moment diagrams**
:::

### Step 1: Calculate Reaction Forces

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal reaction force calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Sum of vertical forces (‚Üë positive):**
   
   $$\sum F_y = 0: R_y - 75(2.5) - 1200 = 0$$
   
   **Reasoning:** 
   - $R_y$ = upward reaction (positive)
   - $75(2.5) = 187.5\ N$ = total distributed load (downward, negative) ‚úÖ
   - $1200\ N$ = point load (downward, negative) ‚úÖ
   
   $$R_y = 187.5 + 1200 = 1387.5 \text{ N (upward)}$$ ‚úÖ

2. **Sum of moments about base (counterclockwise positive):**
   
   $$\sum M_{base} = 0: M_{base} - 75(2.5)(1.25) - 1200(2.5) = 0$$
   
   **Reasoning:** 
   - $M_{base}$ = base reaction moment (counterclockwise, positive)
   - $75(2.5)(1.25) = 234.375\ N¬∑m$ = distributed load moment (clockwise, negative) ‚úÖ
   - $1200(2.5) = 3000\ N¬∑m$ = point load moment (clockwise, negative) ‚úÖ
   
   $$M_{base} = 234.375 + 3000 = 3234.375 \text{ N¬∑m (counterclockwise)}$$ ‚úÖ

3. **Verify equilibrium:**
   
   Forces: ‚Üë1387.5 N = ‚Üì(187.5 + 1200) N ‚úÖ
   Moments: 3234.375 N¬∑m = 3234.375 N¬∑m ‚úÖ

</Steps>

</details>

### Step 2: Construct Shear Force Diagram

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal shear force analysis**</summary>

**Method:** Cut the beam at distance x from base and analyze equilibrium.

**For 0 ‚â§ x ‚â§ 2.5 m:**

**Applying the cantilever shear force equation (see theory section):**
$$V(x) = -(75 \times 2.5 + 1200) + 75x = -1387.5 + 75x$$

**Key points:**
- At x = 0: V(0) = -1387.5 N (negative, as expected for downward loads) ‚úÖ
- At x = 2.5 m: V(2.5) = -1387.5 + 75(2.5) = -1200 N ‚úÖ

**Jump at tip from point load:** <br/>
Just before tip: V = -1200 N <br/>
Just after tip: V = -1200 + 1200 = 0 N ‚úÖ

- **Negative values throughout** due to downward loading
- **Linear increase** (becomes less negative) due to distributed load being "taken up"

### Step 3: Construct Bending Moment Diagram

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal bending moment analysis**</summary>

**Method:** Integrate shear force or use moment equilibrium.

**For 0 ‚â§ x ‚â§ 2.5 m:**

**Applying the cantilever bending moment equation (see theory section):**
$$M(x) = -3234.375 + 1387.5x - 37.5x^2$$

**Key points:**
- At x = 0: M(0) = -3234.375 N¬∑m (negative = sagging moment at fixed end) ‚úÖ
- At x = 2.5 m: M(2.5) = -3234.375 + 1387.5(2.5) - 37.5(2.5)¬≤ = -3234.375 + 3468.75 - 234.375 = 0 N¬∑m ‚úÖ

**Free end boundary condition:**
At the free end (x = 2.5 m), the internal moment must be zero since there are no applied moments. Our equation gives M(2.5) = 0, confirming equilibrium at the tip ‚úÖ

</details>

### Step 4: Stress Analysis and Safety Assessment

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal stress analysis and safety factor calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Maximum shear location:**

   Maximum |V| = 1387.5 N at x = 0 (fixed support) ‚úÖ

2. **Maximum moment location:**

   For cantilever beams with downward loading, the maximum moment typically occurs at the **fixed support**. Let's verify by checking the boundary values:

   - At x = 0 (support): |M(0)| = 3234.375 N¬∑m = 3,234,375 N¬∑mm ‚úÖ
   - At x = 2.5 m (tip): |M(2.5)| = 0 N¬∑m ‚úÖ

   **Maximum |M|** = 3,234,375 N¬∑mm at x = 0 (fixed support) ‚úÖ

3. **Maximum bending stress calculation:**

   Using the flexure formula: $$\sigma_{max} = \frac{M_{max} \cdot c}{I}$$

   Where:
   - $M_{max} = 3,234,375$ N¬∑mm
   - $c = 40$ mm (distance from neutral axis to extreme fiber)
   - $I = 5.33 \times 10^6$ mm‚Å¥

   $$\sigma_{max} = \frac{3,234,375 \times 40}{5.33 \times 10^6} = \frac{129,375,000}{5,330,000} = 24.3 \text{ MPa}$$ ‚úÖ

4. **Safety factor against yield:**

   $$SF = \frac{\sigma_{yield}}{\sigma_{max}} = \frac{275}{24.3} = 11.3$$ ‚úÖ

   **Engineering Assessment:** Safety factor of 11.3 is very conservative and indicates over-design. For robotic applications, SF = 2-4 is typically adequate.

5. **Design recommendations:**

   **Option 1:** Reduce cross-section to 60 mm √ó 8 mm for weight optimization while maintaining SF ‚â• 3

   **Option 2:** Use hollow rectangular section (80√ó10 mm outer, 60√ó6 mm inner) for 40% weight reduction

   **Option 3:** Implement variable cross-section with maximum at base, tapering toward tip

</Steps>

</details>

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal critical analysis and design recommendations**</summary>

<Steps>

   **Center (x = 1000 mm):** M = 520 N¬∑m ‚úÖ
   **Under loads (x = 600, 1400 mm):** M = 400 N¬∑m ‚úÖ

   **Critical location:** Center of beam (midspan) despite no load at this point ‚úÖ

2. **Maximum bending stress:**

   $$\sigma_{max} = \frac{M_{max} \cdot c}{I} = \frac{520,000 \times 30}{3.1 \times 10^6} = 50.3 \text{ MPa}$$ ‚úÖ

4. **Engineering insight - Midspan vs Under-Load Comparison:**

   **Why midspan is critical:**
   - In simply supported beams with multiple point loads, moment builds up between loads
   - **Midspan moment (520 N¬∑m) > Under-load moment (400 N¬∑m)**
   - This occurs because the center experiences cumulative effect of loads on both sides

   **Design recommendation:** Size beam based on midspan moment, not just load locations ‚úÖ

5. **Practical implications:**

   - **Deflection** will also be maximum at center
   - **Lateral-torsional buckling** check needed at center span
   - Consider **intermediate supports** if moment is excessive

</Steps>

### Step 1: Calculate Reaction Forces

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal reaction force calculations**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Sum of vertical forces (‚Üë positive):**

   $$\sum F_y = 0: R_A + R_B - w \cdot L_{total} - P = 0$$

   $$R_A + R_B = 300 \times 3.0 + 600 = 900 + 600 = 1500 \text{ N}$$ ‚úÖ

2. **Sum of moments about A (counterclockwise positive):**

   $$\sum M_A = 0: R_B(2.5) - w \cdot L_{total} \cdot \frac{L_{total}}{2} - P(3.0) = 0$$

   $$R_B(2.5) = 300 \times 3.0 \times 1.5 + 600 \times 3.0$$

   $$R_B(2.5) = 1350 + 1800 = 3150$$

   $$R_B = \frac{3150}{2.5} = 1260 \text{ N}$$ ‚úÖ

3. **Calculate R_A:**

   $$R_A = 1500 - 1260 = 240 \text{ N}$$ ‚úÖ

4. **Verification by moments about B:**

   $$\sum M_B = 0: -R_A(2.5) + w \times 2.5 \times 1.25 - w \times 0.5 \times (2.5 + 0.25) - P \times 0.5 = 0$$

   $$-240(2.5) + 300 \times 2.5 \times 1.25 - 300 \times 0.5 \times 2.75 - 600 \times 0.5$$

   $$= -600 + 937.5 - 412.5 - 300 = -375 + 937.5 - 712.5 = -375 + 225 = -150$$
